The web has become like an online University with courses in every discipline. In order to teach effectively it is necessary to learn and follow guidelines set up for web developers in general. If the internet is like a highway, and websites are like destinations along the highway, a course delivered on a website must meet the standards and laws set down to ensure a safe destination.

What is Web Accessibility

The term Accessbility, in general, means that people are not excluded from taking part in an activity because they are disabled in some manner. Disabilities can be visual, aural, musculo-skeletal, cognitive or mental. When referring to accessbility on the web, the term translated into creating web pages that can be used efficiently by disabled people without them having to provide to much adpation.

The web is an audio visual medium and interaction is provide by keyboard, mouse or touch screen. As content deliverers on the web, eLearning instructors need to provide way for disabilities such as poor vision or hearing to be addressed. The CDC reports that:

Approximately 12 million people 40 years and over in the United States have vision impairment, including 1 million who are blind, 3 million who have vision impairment after correction, and 8 million who have vision impairment due to uncorrected refractive error.

The US Department of Health and Human Services reports that

Approximately 15% of American adults (37.5 million) aged 18 and over report some trouble hearing.

People with certain nerve and musculo-skeletal disabilities are not able to operate a mouse or touch device. Peoplse with cognitive or mental disorders may have difficulty focusing and getting information from complex visual/audio prentations that may be served up from a web page.

For all of the disabilities listed above and others not listed, web accessibility guidelines help to make it possible for information on a web page be made available to all who come to it.

Universal Design and UI/UX

Universal Design is a process for creating products that can be used by all people, including those with disabilities. UI/UX is a process for creating usable web pages and web applications. This means that they are easy to learn, consistent and efficient. Both of these disciplines overlap with Web Accessbility. We can borrow from these disciplines to create eLearning web pages that are easy to learn from for disabled as well as non-disabled students.

Web Accessbility Guidelines and the Law

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), are provided by the W3C (World Wide Web Consortion) in its Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI). Currently the W3C is on version 2 of these guidelines and they provide many resources for coding, testing and obtaining an accessible web page

In addition to the web providing it's own guidelines, other organizations have input:

  • The US Departement of Justice provides a toolkit to support web creation per the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)
  • Section 508, which mandates that all federal agencies make websites available to people with disabilities. This applies to companies and educational institutions that receive federal grant money as well as federal workplaces.

A number of lawsuits have been filed over the years that are related to websites that don't provide access for people with disabilities. In 2008, Target settled a class action lawsuite for $6,000,000. A blind student at UC Berkeley was unable to use their website because it did not operate well with screen readers. The image tags did not contain the alt attribute and therefore there was no text explaining what was in the image.
