Accessibility on the Web for eLearning

Accessible Images

As you may recall from the introduction to the course, Target settled a $6 billion lawsuit and it was because they had not included alt tags to explain what was in their images. Many websites that serve up a lot of images will automate the process of providing alt tags. An image tag should look like this:

<img src="path/to/img-of-a-cat.jpg" alt="Image of a cat">

Accessible Video

To make video accessible you need to create subtitles and/or captions. Subtitles are words and they are baked in to the video, which means you don't turn them on an off. You need an .SRT (SubRip Subtitle ) file to create subtitles. You can also use a .VTT file for subtitles and it can be used for captions, aka "Closed Captioned". Captions differ from subtitle in that they may include text for sounds like "romantic music playing the background", whereas subtitles are just spoken text. Closed captioned access is also usually provided by clicking on a button to turn it on an off. Both of these are text files that you can edit. The text is associated with points on a timeline so that the text can be show to match the video timeline.
